About Us

Promoting Stewardship and Education

Our park protects the northernmost stand of naturally occurring bald cypress trees. Most of these trees are between 70- and 200-years-old. Click the following link to learn more about the bald cypress.

Guests of all ages are welcome to tour the park. Stroll along the winding, quarter-mile boardwalk to experience these unique wetlands. Need a trail map?

Don’t forget to check out the meadow. This area has a diverse collection of tree species planted along the trails. Springtime brings an explosion of wildflower blooms too.

A Sanctuary for More Than Just Trees

Battle Creek Cypress Swamp is home to three disabled birds. Visit the aviary page to learn more about these birds of prey.

You can also find frogs and turtles living inside the Nature Center (currently closed due to fire damage).

The History of Battle Creek Cypress Swamp

The current 100 acre preserve began in 1957. A lot happened before then.

  • Late 1600s- Robert Brooke gave Battle Creek its name as a tribute to his late wife. Battle was the name of her hometown.
  • 1900s- Many bald cypress trees were cut down for lumber.
  • 1900s- The Grays family used the swamp to collect ice in the winter. They stored it and made ice cream in the summer. They also provided housing in exchange for farming labor. You can see a tenant house commissioned by Lawrence Gray in the image to the left.
  • 1940s and 1950s- Two scientists, Romeo Mansueti and Flippo Gravatt noted the rarity of the swamp’s ecosystem.
  • 1957- The Nature Conservancy purchased the land.
  • 1965- It was designated a National Natural Landmark.
  • 1977- Boardwalk construction began. The sanctuary was leased to Calvert County, MD and began operation as a county park.
